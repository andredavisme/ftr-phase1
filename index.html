A Note for Joe

This plan was built with one thing in mind: your time is the most valuable ingredient in the kitchen. Everything here is designed to work for a solo operator — mostly running on its own once it's set up, with only a small amount of your attention each week. No tech background needed. No big budget required. Just a few smart moves that will get Fork in the Road known, loved, and busy in Portland.

🎯

What We're Trying to Do

🤝 Serve the Community

Build a reputation as the pizza spot that genuinely gives back to Portland — veterans, students, neighbors.

📈 Bring in More Customers

Get more people through the door consistently — not just on random good days — throughout the whole year.

💰 Improve Profit Margins

Target a 5% improvement in what you keep after costs, by designing smart combo deals that protect your margins.

You're open Monday–Friday, 10am–3pm. You run the place yourself, with occasional help from a family member. Hiring part-time help is a goal 6–8 months out. The two businesses next door — Dunkin' and City Deli — already have their lanes. Your lane is community, craft, and character. That's what this plan builds on.

The Programs — Set It & Let It Run

These are called passive programs — meaning you set them up once and they keep working without you needing to do anything extra each day.

Passive · Start Now

🪙 Pay-It-Forward Wall

A customer pays for a future stranger's meal — you put their name or a note on a board. The next person who needs it claims it. It costs you nothing to run, creates a talking point, and gives people a reason to share your restaurant online and with friends.

  • A simple chalkboard, corkboard with index cards, or your existing in-store TV can all work
  • Customers write a note, you pin it up — the next claimant takes one down
  • Optional: add a raffle jar so each donated meal also enters a fun drawing
Recommended approach: Use the chalkboard or corkboard for the warm, personal feel — and your existing TV screen (already installed, no extra cost) to display a running tally like "14 meals donated this month." A small tablet ($150–$300 one-time) can update that screen remotely.
Passive · Start Now

🎖️ Veteran Thank-You Combo

A fixed-price meal designed to say thank you to veterans — no ID check needed. You simply create a "Veteran Combo" (e.g. a slice + drink at a set price) and post a sign. Anyone who identifies as a veteran orders it. Trust in community is part of your brand.

  • Build the combo around ingredients that keep your cost predictable — not a percentage off random items
  • A static sign on the counter is all you need. Post once, done.
  • Major restaurant chains like Mission BBQ use this exact honor-based approach
Passive · Start Now

🎒 Student Combo

A simple, affordable pre-set meal aimed at students and young workers in the building and nearby. Available every day — no extra prep, no separate rush to manage. Priced at a flat dollar amount so your costs stay predictable.

  • One or two options max — e.g. "1 slice + small drink" at a fixed price
  • Pre-portion ingredients during slow periods so service stays fast during rushes
  • Post a small sign and list it on your social media once — it runs itself
Passive · Start Now

🏢 Portland Business Lunch Program

A standing lunch deal for office workers in One City Center and nearby buildings. You send one email to building management — they forward it to their tenants. After that, it runs on word of mouth and a simple discount code.

  • Give the code a memorable name (something tied to the building or Portland)
  • No printed cards or apps needed — just something people remember and say out loud
  • Reaches office workers who are already in your building and looking for a lunch option
Passive · Future Opportunities

🎬 Nickelodeon Cinema Partnership

The Nickelodeon Cinema is right across the street. A simple cross-promotion — "Show your ticket stub, get a deal" — ties movie nights to Fork in the Road. This is a conversation to start, not a day-one project.

Passive · Future Opportunities

📻 Radio Station Relationships (WBLM & WCYY)

You already have a connection with staff at both stations. That relationship is worth nurturing. A shoutout on local radio — even informal — reaches thousands of Portland-area listeners. Keep the door open and let it happen naturally.

Seasonal

🏕️ Summer Camp Meal Program

Off-site meal prep for summer camps is a real revenue opportunity with no walk-in traffic required. This is a mid-year goal — plan for June–August when the program is in the seasonal calendar.

Seasonal

🍂 Community Fundraiser Nights

Partner with a local organization — school, nonprofit, neighborhood group — for one night where a portion of sales supports their cause. They promote it to their audience; you get traffic. Plan one per quarter to start.

Social Media — Simple & Steady Wins

Right now Fork in the Road has almost no online presence. The goal isn't to go viral — it's to show up consistently so that when someone in Portland searches for pizza or a lunch spot, you're there.

📘 Facebook & Instagram

Your main channel for reaching regular customers — locals, office workers, students, families. Post the same content on both platforms at the same time. Think of these as your community bulletin board.

  • Show off your food with a simple phone photo — natural light, no fancy camera needed
  • Highlight your programs (pay-it-forward milestones, student combo, veteran combo)
  • Share moments: a busy lunch rush, a fun customer story, a seasonal special

💼 LinkedIn

LinkedIn is where Portland businesses and office workers live online. Use it to reach building managers, corporate lunch buyers, and local business owners. Think of it as your B2B handshake.

  • Post the Business Lunch Program discount as a "corporate perk" businesses can share with employees
  • Send a personal connection request to One City Center management with a short friendly note
  • One post per month on LinkedIn is enough to start building visibility

Your Actual Time Commitment

This whole plan is built to ask as little of your time as possible. Here's what it actually looks like week to week:

1×
mo

Monthly Batch Session — ~2 Hours

Once a month, sit down and write out all your posts for the coming month. Schedule them in advance using a free tool like Meta Business Suite (Facebook/Instagram's own free scheduler). Do it all at once — like prep work before a big service.

1×
wk

Weekly Post — ~5 Minutes

Same day, same time each week (e.g. every Wednesday at 10am) — post whichever pre-written update is ready for that week. Building a consistent rhythm trains your audience to expect you and keeps your name in their feed.

as
needed

Real-Time Moments — Whenever They Happen

A big pay-it-forward milestone. A radio shoutout from WBLM. A great customer moment. Post these when they happen — they don't disrupt the plan, they add to it. These organic moments are often the most engaging content of all.

Total planned time per month: One 2-hour session + a few minutes each week to post. Everything else runs on its own.

Next Steps — In Order

Don't try to do everything at once. Here's a suggested order of operations for your first 30 days:

Week 1 — Set Up the Foundation

  • Put up the Pay-It-Forward board in-store (chalkboard or corkboard — your call)
  • Write the Veteran Combo and Student Combo onto your menu board or a small counter sign
  • Create your Facebook, Instagram, and LinkedIn pages if not already done

Week 2 — Reach Out

  • Email One City Center building management with your Business Lunch discount code
  • Send a LinkedIn connection request to building management and any known Portland business contacts
  • Post an introduction on Facebook/Instagram — who you are, what Fork in the Road is about

Week 3–4 — Get Into Rhythm

  • Do your first monthly batch session: write 4 posts for the coming month, schedule them
  • Pick your weekly posting day and stick to it
  • Walk across the street and introduce yourself to Nickelodeon Cinema management

Month 2 and Beyond

  • Follow the seasonal calendar — adjust based on what's working
  • Track which programs bring in the most new faces and lean into those
  • Around month 6, revisit the idea of part-time help — by then, the programs will be generating enough traffic to justify it
